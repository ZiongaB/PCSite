Where do I start with computers?

Getting a new computer can be a complicated process when you don't know what you need! Getting the wrong computer for your needs can be expensive and frustrating.


Besides cost there are a lot of factors to consider. What will you be using it for primarily? What level of power do you want? Do you need to take it on the go? Do you want to be able to modify or upgrade it?


Below we provide some information to help answer some of these questions.

What type of machine do I need?

When it comes to your needs, the form factor of a computer is one of the most important ones. Let's talk about the main type of machines to consider.

Desktops

Tower-based desktop computers, despite becoming less popular compared to laptops, are still often what people would think of when asked to envision the word "computer".


These computers have all of the computing hardware held within the tower (a self contained case) that plugs into a separate monitor/tv and keyboard in order to function.

Pros:

  • Performance: The size and design of a tower PC is conducive to having the most modern and powerful hardware you can afford.
  • Upgrading: Due to the large, modular nature of desktop PCs and their parts, allow for easy upgrading and replacing of single parts.
  • Cost: Because desktops are modular, they are easier to repair/replace parts for, saving you money.
  • Customization: Peripherals and your computer's setup are lot easier to customize when everything isn't all in one piece.

Cons:

  • Space requirements: Because of their larger size compared to laptops, desktops tend to require more space.
  • Portability: Transporting a large pc tower around is impractical, meaning it's better suited for staying in one place.
  • Peripherals: Desktops don't always come with monitors, keyboards, mice, or other peripherals, which must be bought separately.


Laptops

Laptops computers are probably the more common form of computer used by the average person nowadays. Their portable nature makes them less customizable, but more versatile in when and where they're used.

Pros:

  • Portability: Laptops are designed to go wherever you need them to! This allows for a much more flexibility of use.
  • All-in-one: Laptops come with all the necessary parts and peripherals built in, which makes for a simpler user experience.
  • Energy Efficiency: Laptops are designed to run off battery power, and therefore tend to be energy efficient compared to desktops.

Cons:

  • Cost: The cost of being a portable all-in-one computer means for comparable desktops, laptops tend to be more expensive.
  • Upgrading: Laptops generally have little to no room for upgrades or to replace specific parts that a desktop normally allows.
  • Performance: Due to their limited size and design specifications, laptops have more limited power and performance compared to desktops.


All-in-one Desktop

A category of computer that combines aspects of desktop and laptop design to create something more streamlined than a desktop, but not as portable as a laptop.

Pros:

  • Form Factor: All-in-one desktops tend to be more compact, clean, and with fewer cables than your average desktop PC.
  • Transportation It's easier to transport a single device instead of a desktop PC tower, monitor, and peripherals.

Cons:

  • Cost: Much like laptops, a machine designed be all-in-one tends to cost more than the equivalent custom-built desktops.
  • Power: Due to the more compact nature of all-in-ones, they tend to have less power than similar desktops.
  • Lack of Modification Similar to laptops, they tend to be much harder to repair, and replacing parts tends to not be an option.

The heck is an operating system?

An Operating System (OS) is the software that runs your computer. It's how you interact with files, programs, and the hardware that makes up the computer.

There are a variety of options for computer users, but let's look at the most common ones.

Windows

Windows is the most commonly used operating system on modern computers. Made by Microsoft, it's easy to use and a versatile OS.

Pros:

  • Ease of Use: Windows is generally regarded as easy to navigate for a beginner, and has plenty of resources to learn how to use it.
  • Hardware Compatibility: Because Windows is so common, large amounts of modern computing hardware is built with it in mind. Compatibility for Windows will not be an issue on most hardware.
  • Software Compatibility: Similarly, a huge chunk of modern software is built with Windows in mind. Most games and programs you intend to use will run on a Windows machine.

Cons:

  • System Requirements: Modern versions of Windows can be somewhat resource and storage intensive, with weaker performance on older hardware.
  • Vulnerability: Because it's so commonly used, viruses and malware are a lot more common for Windows devices.
  • Prices: Many programs for windows like Photoshop can be expensive, and the price of a license for Windows itself can be pricey if it doesn't come with the computer.


Mac

MacOS is probably the second most commonly used OS in personal computers. Developed by Apple, it's streamlined and designed for ease of use and interconnectivity with Apple devices.

Pros:

  • Ease of Use: Much like Windows, MacOS tends to be easy to use and learn. It may even be more streamlined than Windows.
  • Security: Because it's less used than Windows, less viruses and malware are designed to attack MacOS.
  • Ecosystem: The Mac ecosystem means that connecting your Mac computer to other devices like IPhones and IPads are very simple.

Cons:

  • Software Compatibility: Due to being less used, many programs designed for Windows will not work on Mac. This includes many video games.
  • Customization: On a software level, MacOS is more simplified than other OS's and allows for less customization/modification at a software level.
  • Limited Hardware Options MacOS usually only comes on Apple computers like Macbooks. This means if you want MacOS you have to buy an Apple device.


Linux

Linux is an open-source operating system that has been around since the 90's. While it's less common, it is used by all sorts of devices besides personal computers. It can be found running servers, cars, and all sorts of other electronic devices.

Pros:

  • Open Source: Linux is free to use, modify, and distribute. This means anyone can create their own custom version of it and share it freely./li>
  • Extensive Customization: Linux allows for extensive customization at a code level that allows the user to modify their system to their heart's content.
  • Security/Stability: Linux is reliable, fast, and secure. It's used in servers and supercomputers all over the world. It runs efficiently, and can handle high workloads for extended periods of time.

Cons:

  • Learning Curve: Linux is much harder to learn, often requiring understanding of command line to install and run programs, modify files, and perform various actions.
  • Hardware Compatibility: Not all hardware is compatible with Linux, meaning research is required to make sure you know your computer can run it.
  • Software Compatibility: Because Linux is less popular for personal use, many common Windows programs do not run on it.
